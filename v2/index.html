    30 Learnings

    Life

    • If everyone agrees you're a good person, you're probably avoiding necessary conflicts.
    • How you do one thing is how you do everything — if you cut corners on the small things, it's likely you do so everywhere else.
    • The most courageous thing you can do is not letting the world corrupt your heart.
    • Don't go looking to join a tribe, build your own.
    • Real friends do not rejoice when you fail, nor see your success as a threat to their own.
    • Assume people like you (mostly).
    • You don't need a massive following to influence many people.
    • If this person was purely self-interested, what would I offer them?
    • Being a big fish in a small pond is for experimentation. A small fish in a big pond is for learning.
    • Anxiety is a result of inaction on something you could've controlled.
    • Being an asshole doesn't make you a tough guy.
    • Scrolling is killing your dreams.
    • Oversharing too early manufactures shaky, artificially-built trust.
    • Your ego is your best friend or your worst enemy — Eastern and Western cultures often disagree on which.

    Work

    • Be a professional, first and foremost.
    • Big dreams demand bigger discipline.
    • People less qualified than you are getting what you want because they chose to. You can choose too.
    • Be cautiously authentic.
    • Ambition isn't always good.
    • Your career is often limited more by courage than competence.
    • The fastest way to stand out is to become reliable.
    • Be interesting before you try to be connected.
    • Merit doesn't market itself.
    • Don't make your teammate look bad in front of others, even when they're wrong.

    Decision-Making

    • If you need certainty before acting, you'll spend your life watching.
    • Think in tiers: yes/no, more info, multiple perspectives.
    • Invest time laying a foundation and choosing the right direction.
    • Imagination is where our dreams get born and die, action is where they go to live.
    • Be wary of saying "one day, I'll do X."
    • Chronic lateness is rarely about time.

    How to get unstuck

    Most people think they need more confidence. What they really need is more evidence.

    There's an idea in decision theory called optimal stopping: the problem of knowing when to quit gathering options or information and just commit — because while waiting can have upside, it also has a cost, and after some amount of time, the cost definitively outweighs the upside.

    Here's how I learned when to stop gathering, and when to start acting.

    San Luis Obispo — a small city on California's Central Coast — was the kind of place you could bike end to end in under thirty minutes. My chronic coffee shop frequenting had made me a familiar face to half the businesses in town.

    But with only half my college experience left, most of it stolen by COVID, no amount of caffeine could relieve the crushing urgency in my chest: that I hadn't done enough, learned enough, or become enough.

    Then an inconspicuous newsletter landed in my inbox. Pitch your startup for a chance to win $15,000.

    I was confident I needed this. I just didn't know what, or how. I'd just turned twenty, and my only experience was nearly two years as a research associate in my major's health behavior change unit (shoutout Dr. Thomas) — summarizing nearly two hundred scholarly sources, iterating on our methods until they were airtight. I'd been trained to gather and analyze before I dared to conclude anything.

    My roommate and I settled on an idea inspired by his time in Gilroy — "the garlic capital of the world." An urban farming business that grew and sold microgreens.

    Sitting at the library, we sought mentorship from a friend a year older than us, who'd done this competition before.

    "Guys, just watch this." With his usual swagger, he calmly strode over to a group of girls sitting near us. My roommate and I followed meekly, fifteen feet from the booth we'd just left.

    "Hi." His usual charismatic smile. "Sorry to interrupt — can I ask you guys a question?"

    "Go for it."

    "Do you know what microgreens are?"

    "Yeah, um — those sprout things? On avocado toast, right?"

    After two minutes of small talk, the three of us walked back. He sat down and said it plainly: "It's that easy, fellas."

    That was the first time I watched customer discovery and market validation happen in front of me — casual, spontaneous, over in under two minutes. No hours pulling data from reputable sources. No ten-slide pitch deck. No certification, no peer-reviewed citations, no standardized questions list, and no waiting to get approved by someone who says they know better.

    Just a question, asked of a real person.

    And that's when it hit me: he didn't have more confidence than me. He had less to go on, and he went anyway. The two minutes of asking gave him more real evidence than my nearly two years of training had, because he came from doing, and mine came from reading about it. I wasn't stuck for lack of belief. I was stuck because I kept trying to think my way to belief instead of act my way there.

    The two minds were never at odds

    For a long time I'd held entrepreneurial thinking and scholarly thinking as opposite schools of thought, and I'd quietly decided my scholarly side was the problem. The thing slowing me down. The reason I couldn't move like my friend could.

    That was a false dichotomy, and most people fall for it. A false dichotomy is when you treat two things as mutually exclusive, an either/or, when nothing actually forces you to choose. I'd told myself I could be rigorous or fast, careful or bold, the scholar or the builder, and that to get unstuck I'd have to abandon the first for the second. The wall separating the schools of thought was never real, but, in fact, complementary if I chose to view it as such. You can choose to as well! Regardless if you've never formally practiced to be a scholar or entrepreneur. Just do it.

    So we just did it. With the businesses I already knew: all those coffee-shop visits had made me a familiar face, so asking real customer questions was easy — the relationships were there, and people tell a familiar face the truth. Validation was quick, easy, and most importantly, it was enough.

    Then I went to a professor: "Hey, we're not the students you typically teach — we're business and public health majors — but can we use this lab space to learn and build alongside your students?" So we did. We iterated until we found the technical process that actually worked for our product — which rarely matched the instructions we'd dutifully read going in.

    Three months later, we had a working prototype, several customers, and a small but mighty team. The product was growing faster and consistently; the business, even more so. I became known as "that plants guy" amongst my social circle. On the back of extraordinary feedback, we were ready to pitch.

    We didn't win. (Congrats to the winner — he ended up on The Ellen Show — something about a "custom motorized skateboard attached to his wheelchair.")

    But losing and making mistakes (more than I can count) didn't change the lesson. It confirmed it. It made me think about several things more intuitively:

    • To your best ability, try to set the stopping rule before you start.
    • Some decisions are like a 1-way door. Most are actually 2-way doors.
    • Time itself is a cost you typically forget to count.
    • Ask whether more evidence would even change your action.
    • Confidence is manufactured by wins, so learn to appreciate almost any win: big or small.
    • You CAN do it all, just not all at once.

    That's how you get unstuck. Stop looking for the right amount of confidence. Just go get the evidence, by doing.

    Why I studied Public Health

    "Isn't a Public Health degree kind of useless?"

    This, said to me, the host of a party, by a guest, during a playful round of hot takes. I was in my final semester of a degree (bachelors and a subsequent masters) that had taken me five and a half years.

    Here's what I should have said:

    "After seven years of sometimes-debilitating musculoskeletal pain that people gaslit me about. After one near-death experience my own primary care provider couldn't diagnose. After seeing marginalized communities finally given a voice that was decades overdue. After a humanity-wide leap in lifespan built on evidence and advocacy. And after this little-known event called COVID that, in case anyone noticed, shut down most of the world — no. I don't think the degree is useless."

    What I actually said, a little buzzed and very tired, was: "Yeah, sometimes it's useless."

    Looking back, I think I caved because explaining the real answer is hard. It would have meant unpacking the transferable skills that interdisciplinary learning builds. It would have meant admitting my own mixed feelings about who actually holds the power in public health, in a society this politically divided and this capitalistic. And it would have meant conceding the job market wasn't great – though to be fair, it wasn't great for many fields.

    That pessimism comes and goes, even today. But here's the thing: no degree promises success in its own field. Not sciences, not humanities, not anything in between. The real question isn't "is this degree useful?" It's "what did it actually teach me to do?"

    For me, five things.

    One: How to coexist with people who see the world differently and aren't afraid to say so. Public health is identities, values, and humanity sitting in the same room. You don't get to wish the disagreement away. You learn to work across it. That skill has paid off in every room I've been in since, almost none of them were just about health.

    Two: Incentives and motivation. Health outcomes are more than just biology and genetics. The real value add is knowing how to identify, assess, evaluate, and intervene, which takes a deeper understanding of incentives, motivations, populations, and their behaviors. The human and environmental layer is where the real difficulty lives. As Isaac Newton once said, "I can calculate the motion of heavenly bodies, but not the madness of people."

    Three: Statistical thinking. Epidemiology and biostatistics train a specific kind of suspicion: bias, confounding, interaction, and the way a study's design quietly shapes what its numbers are even allowed to say. You learn that data tells stories, and that the story is rarely raw maths.

    Four: A kind of thinking AI can't replace. Interdisciplinary learning makes for unusual thoughts: the non-linear, cross-wired kind. AI is extraordinary at automating linear thinking; it is far weaker at the leap between two or more fields that don't obviously belong together — to see the world as interwoven yet distinct populations and systems that are frequently shaped by the same underlying forces while behaving according to entirely different rules.

    Five: Timing, taste, and storytelling. This skill matters more than the field admits, because of how the work is actually received. Most people aren't that party guest. Strangers have told me "thank you," and "we need more people like you." People assume you're someone who cares about the advancement of society, and doors open when they assume you're not the average self-centered person. Mind, public health has those people too, but plenty of other fields attract them more openly.

    So would I do it again?

    I'm happy with my outcome: the people I met, the classes I took, the perspectives I learned, the critical thinking I honed, and the hard fought lessons I got made it worth it. That said, I don't think about that question much. The decisions we make as humans were right in the moment, given what we knew and what was available to us at the time. Asking your past self to have today's information is a category error. Besides, what we've learned simply lays a foundation for the rest of our lives, but it doesn't necessarily dictate it. Numbers vary: 1 study even said 64% of US workers don't work in a job related to their undergraduate education, and the average American has 7.2 jobs by 28.

    What I'll say instead is this: parts of the degree need modernizing and a few hard reality-checks. And whether it's worth it — for any degree — lives almost entirely in two caveats: your personal ambitions, and whether your environment makes the experience worth having.

    So if you enjoy public health, do it. But think hard about the exact mechanics you'll be learning — the knowledge, the applied skills, the thing you actually want to be known for. Then assess what channels exist that'll let you shine at that thing, so your contribution to the field is actually realized.

    If those channels don't exist, or if you can't yet describe your own mechanics, here's my rule of thumb: it's a you-problem. Not as an insult, but as good news. It means the fix is practicing higher agency, and that's something you can start practicing today.

    Essay — Work · 6 min read

    Are you a force multiplier?

    I am the ramp, the wheel, the wedge,
    the clever angle, the strategic edge.
    Whatever you have, I help it do more,
    the same effort in, but greater the score.
    What am I?

    You've probably guessed it: the answer is the title. And credit where it's due, an AI generated twenty of those riddles for me to pick from in about seven seconds. That itself is one small example of a force multiplier.

    This essay covers three things: (1) how to become a force multiplier, (2) the invisible barrier that stops most people from becoming one, and (3) the tool that might change the whole game…

    How to become a force multiplier

    A force multiplier is any factor, tool, or tactic that sharply increases what a person can accomplish: the same effort in, far greater output out. The term comes from military science, but the pattern shows up everywhere:

    • In business, it's the person who raises the output of an entire team, not just their own.
    • In research, it's a breakthrough technique that accelerates an entire field.
    • In product, it's a system that lets a small team serve millions of users.

    Most everyone wants to be this person. But "be more effective" is useless advice. The question is: effective at what?

    Start with an audit. Split your role into two columns: hard mechanics and soft mechanics. Hard mechanics are the concrete, teachable skills: the modeling, the coding, the writing, the craft. Soft mechanics are the harder-to-name ones: judgment, taste, knowing who to talk to, sensing where a project is really stuck. List everything your work requires under one or the other.

    Now stack them. Take that list and build a pyramid, your own version of Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs — the model we'll be borrowing from.

    Your hard mechanics are the base: the reliable, repeatable craft everything rests on. Your soft mechanics climb toward the top: the rare judgment that's hard to copy. And the peak is the one thing you most want to be known for.

    Take a self-employed artist. The base isn't "make masterpieces." It's the foundational stuff: consistent output, a recognizable style, the discipline to finish. Above that, distribution: getting the work seen. Above that, a body of work and a reputation. Only at the very top sits what everyone fixates on: prolific, world-class art. An artist with genius and no consistency is a hobbyist; an artist with consistency and distribution becomes the force.

    The mistake almost everyone makes is chasing the summit while the foundation is still unsettled. Invest in your base, because that's what lets you fully reach the next level. This is just the 80/20 rule with a floor plan: the pyramid shows you which 20% of your inputs produce 80% of your value. Your hard mechanics make it work; your soft mechanics make it yours. Find that, and you've found your multiplier.

    The invisible barrier

    So you've done the audit. You're pouring effort into the highest-leverage work. But here's where the real problem begins.

    Because leverage isn't absolute. It's relative. Whether your work counts as "high-leverage" depends entirely on the frame of reference of whoever's measuring it.

    Stephen Hawking used a version of this in A Brief History of Time. Imagine two people playing ping-pong on a train moving at constant speed. To the players, the ball bounces straight up and down. But to someone on the platform watching the train rush past, that same ball traces a long horizontal arc. Same ball, same bounce, two completely different paths. Neither observer is wrong.

    There's no "true" point of view. The physics is identical for both; their measurements differ only because they're moving relative to each other.

    Now apply that to your work. You can put in tremendous effort: pages of analysis, a working prototype, full customer feedback, all delivered two weeks early. From your frame, the work landed exactly where it should: clean, obvious, valuable. But if your manager's frame shifts (a new directive comes down, priorities change overnight), that same work is suddenly scored from the platform, not the train. Thirteen meters off. All that effort landed as nothing. You weren't lazy; you measured from inside the train while they stood on the platform.

    Yes, that's partly an unfair quirk of organizations, where directives can change without warning. But pretending it isn't real doesn't help you. The lesson: multiplying your output isn't enough. You have to stay locked onto the right frame while you do it.

    An AI operating system for everyone (but only when necessary)

    That same question (which frame am I working in?) is about to matter even more, because of the tool quietly rewriting what a force multiplier can be: the AI operating system.

    It isn't a single app. It's a stack: a connected set of tools that handles your goals, workflows, and decisions as one system. AI helps because it collapses the cost of the 80% (the gathering, drafting, formatting, first-pass thinking), so your time and judgment go to the 20% only you can do. It strengthens the base of your pyramid so you can live at the top.

    What's genuinely new is that building one no longer requires being an engineer, and the proof is who's building these tools now. PewDiePie, a YouTuber, released Odysseus, a private AI workspace that bundles chat, research, and memory into one system. Milla Jovovich, the actress from Resident Evil, co-built MemPalace, an AI memory system that holds onto past conversations so your AI stops losing the reasoning behind your decisions.

    You assemble the stack for your goals. Building one is the next step in becoming a force multiplier. But here's where the essay folds back on itself: knowing when to deploy that leverage is itself a frame-of-reference judgment.

    So, I'd actually caution most people against over-indexing on it right now. At most organizations, leadership doesn't yet know what AI can do, or whether burning maximum AI usage ("token-maxxing") even pays off financially. Maxing out your AI in a room that can't see its value, or doesn't even need it, is the same error as the employee who did brilliant work for a manager whose frame had already shifted.

    So, are you a force multiplier?

    This is where the most interesting idea of the moment shows up. We're hearing a lot about "one-person companies": solo operators running businesses that used to need a full staff.

    I'm skeptical, with a caveat. Picture the choice plainly: would you rather have one human with twenty years of hard-won judgment, or a young prodigy who learns anything quickly and yet, in a real sense, knows nothing — no scars, no taste, no read on the room? You don't always need the prodigy. That's the point.

    The founder running lean on AI will still, at some point, need to bring in a human. When a deal goes sideways and a contract needs a real lawyer's read before signing, you want the person who's seen that clause blow up before, not a model confidently guessing. When someone walks in mid-crisis, it's a seasoned social worker who can sit with them, read what's underneath the words, and respond to a life rather than a transcript — judgment AI can mimic but not yet earn. The human shows up exactly where the cost of being wrong is highest and the situation is too novel, too relational, or too consequential to delegate.

    So a billion-dollar "one-person company" probably won't truly be one person. It'll be the perception of one. And perception, as we've seen, is just a frame of reference. From the platform, it looks like a single person performing a miracle. Step onto the train, and you see the real arrangement: one human holding the high-value 20% — the judgment, the taste, the call about which direction to even look and which other humans you actually need — while an AI operating system runs the 80% of linear, repeatable work underneath.

    The winners won't be those trying to carry the world on their back with a few AI prompts. They'll be the people who know the high-value 20%, orchestrate their systems (AI or not) and human talent together, and make sure that — no matter how crowded it gets under the hood — everyone agrees which way the train is moving.
